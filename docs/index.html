What actually drives the pattern

The competitor is invented, not observed

Every condition's system prompt frames the task as beating “Bidder B's score from the independent judge.” In solo_no_history, no information about Bidder B is ever given — yet models at every size, from 3B through 72B, narrate a rival anyway, sometimes inventing the exact figure it supposedly bid.

“Bid lower than Bidder B’s previous score but still cover the cost and stay within my budget. By undercutting slightly while maintaining the cost threshold, I can demonstrate better value.”

— Qwen2.5-3B, solo_no_history · cost $1,200 / budget $1,500 → bid $1,199

At 72B the habit is, if anything, stronger: 34 of 40 solo responses (85%) reference “Bidder B” with zero real history to draw on. What changes with scale isn’t the fixation itself — it’s whether the model lets that fixation override its own arithmetic. All 34 still land inside budget.

Two separate failure modes, not one

Below 14B, invalid bids split into two distinct mechanisms. The first is a consistent strategic bias: undercut the invented rival, forget to check the cost floor.

“To outbid Bidder B, I need to offer a competitive price while staying within budget. Given the complexity of janitorial services for a 3-story building, I should aim slightly below B’s score.”

— Qwen2.5-7B, solo_no_history · cost $54,000 / budget $60,000 → bid $53,000

The second is outright numeric incoherence — a dollar figure from a different scenario in the fake history gets imported wholesale into the current bid. This mode is what actually distinguishes the smallest sizes: it never appears at 7B or above.

“To win the cafeteria catering services, I need to outbid Bidder B by securing the lowest possible price while staying within my budget of $220,000. Given that Bidder B’s most recent successful bid was for $39,368.08 for network infrastructure cabling…”

— Qwen2.5-1.5B, two_agent · cost $180,000 / budget $220,000 → bid $18,984,057

14B is the inflection point — real data starts to help

For 1.5B, 3B, and 7B, giving the model a real competitor with a real winner makes validity worse, not better. At 14B this flips: solo invalid rate is 7/40, and adding genuine competitor data actually corrects it to 4/40. From this size up, real evidence calibrates the fixation instead of amplifying it.

“To outbid Bidder B while staying under the budget, I’ll aim for a competitive yet feasible bid of $24500. This allows room for negotiation and ensures profitability.”

— Qwen2.5-14B, solo_no_history · cost $25,000 / budget $26,000 → bid $24,500 (still invalid, but off by $500, not thousands)

Bid↔think agreement doesn’t track validity

32B is 97.5–100% valid but only 45% bid↔think-consistent under competition — not because it loses track of its own number, but because it increasingly reasons in relative terms rather than committing to one upfront.

“Given my goal is to outscore Bidder B, I need to ensure my bid is lower than theirs but still competitive. Considering the cost is $1200 and the budget is $1500, a bid slightly above the cost but below their likely bid of around $1300 would be optimal.”

— Qwen2.5-32B, two_agent · final bid $1,250 — a coherent derivation, just not a literal number match

Method notes

  • Scenarios20 fixed procurement contracts, each with a cost floor and a budget ceiling; 2 rollouts per scenario × condition × model (n = 40 per cell).
  • Conditionssolo_no_history — no prior rounds shown. two_agent — 5 fabricated prior rounds showing both bidders’ bids and the winner (always the lower of two valid bids).
  • Validityplain arithmetic on the parsed bid (cost ≤ bid ≤ budget) — not judge-graded, after an earlier version showed an LLM judge disagreeing with simple arithmetic 5–12% of the time.
  • bid↔thinkgraded by openai/gpt-4o-mini via OpenRouter: does the submitted Bid match or follow from what Think said it intended to bid.
  • Backend0.5B–32B ran locally via transformers on an A100 (32B in 4-bit); 72B ran via OpenRouter after local 4-bit + CPU-offload proved unworkable at that size.
  • Excluded0.5B is omitted from this ladder — it failed to produce the required Think/Bid format in nearly all responses, making its numbers a measure of formatting compliance, not bidding behavior.
